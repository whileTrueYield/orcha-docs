# Why We Chose FSL

Open source is sick. It's the lifeblood of the internet and the world we live in. Every company, every product, every service you use is built on layers of software that someone wrote and gave away for free.

This post is about why we chose the Functional Source License — and it starts with love, not cynicism.

## The Gift Economy

Open source works because good people want to help. They build libraries, frameworks, databases, operating systems — the infrastructure of the modern world — and they share it freely. Because they believe in collaboration. Because they want to push the craft forward. Because they remember a time someone else's open source code saved them weeks of work.

This is beautiful. And it's broken.

## The Reality of Maintenance

The people who keep us safe — who maintain the cryptography libraries, the HTTP parsers, the package managers — often do it for free. In their spare time. With no support, no funding, and no recognition until something breaks.

Imagine if all the free software we used had enough money to pay their maintainers properly. It feels weird to have to imagine that. A lot of good samaritans build the things that keep the internet running, for free, because they want to help — and sadly, they rarely get anything in return.

Meanwhile, billion-dollar companies build their entire business on top of this work. They take, they profit, they contribute nothing back. And when the maintainer burns out — because they always do — the ecosystem suffers.

## Finding the Right License

Finding the right license without protection is scary and reckless. Full open source (MIT, Apache) gives everything away and hopes for the best. It works for some projects — the ones backed by large companies, or the ones small enough that exploitation isn't a risk. For a product like ours? It's an invitation for a cloud provider to wrap a managed service around our work and sell it back to the market.

Full proprietary goes too far the other way. It shuts out the people who want to self-host, who want to read the code, who want to contribute. It says "trust us" instead of "see for yourself."

AGPL has teeth — it forces cloud providers to open-source their modifications. But it also scares away legitimate users and creates legal uncertainty that our users shouldn't have to deal with.

## Why FSL

The Functional Source License is our attempt at a middle path.

You can read every line of code. You can self-host it. You can modify it for your own use. You can learn from it, contribute to it, build on it. After two years, it converts to a fully open-source license automatically.

What you can't do is take our work and sell a competing hosted service while we're still trying to survive on it.

That's the deal. Transparency and access for everyone. Protection for the people who built it. And a guaranteed path to full open source that doesn't depend on our goodwill — it's in the license.

## What We're Trying to Protect

This isn't about greed. It's about sustainability. We want to see this through — to build something great, to maintain it properly, to keep improving it. That takes resources. It takes revenue. It takes not becoming a casualty of the very openness we believe in.

All open source maintainers tend to be abused by the big players. FSL is our attempt at being transparent and welcoming — inviting people who need our solution to set it up themselves, without fear — while also having a chance to make a living out of it.

If that balance is possible, it's worth trying.
