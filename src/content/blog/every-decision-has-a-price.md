# Every Decision Has a Price — Are You Being Told What It Is?

Planning meetings never work. Not because people are bad at planning, but because you're never facing the consequences of your choices.

## The Silent Killer: "What If"

Every planning meeting has them. "What if we squeeze in this feature?" "What if we pull two people off Project A for a week?" "What if we push the deadline by a sprint?"

These what-ifs are the silent killer. They always sound reasonable — even exciting — when you don't realize their impact. Delivering that extra feature sounds great, until you understand what it actually costs: three other features delayed by two weeks, a release date that slips into the next quarter, a team that's now overcommitted on two fronts.

But nobody in the room can compute that cascade in real time. So the what-if gets a nod, a "let's try it," and the consequences surface weeks later when it's too late to undo them.

## You Can't Make Decisions Without Knowing the Price

We all want to deliver the next great thing. That's not the problem. The problem is making that decision *without knowing what it costs*.

Everything has a price. Every feature you add pushes something else down. Every priority change ripples through the schedule. Every new hire takes time to ramp up, and during that ramp, someone else is mentoring instead of building.

These aren't hidden costs — they're *uncomputed* costs. The information exists. The team's capacity is known. The backlog is known. The dependencies are known. What's missing is the ability to see the cascade *before* you commit.

## The Planning Meeting Illusion

Planning meetings create the illusion of informed decision-making. You've got smart people in a room, discussing trade-offs, drawing on whiteboards. It feels productive. It feels like you're making careful choices.

But without the ability to simulate the impact of those choices, you're making decisions in the dark. You're approving scope changes without understanding their cost. You're reshuffling priorities without seeing what moves as a result.

Planning properly is hard — we've seen in previous posts just how hard. It's hard if not impossible for a human being to do. Now imagine doing it for every "what if" thrown at you in a 60-minute meeting.

## The Fix: Show the Price in Real Time

What if, instead of debating what-ifs in the abstract, you could see their impact in seconds?

Move a feature up in priority — instantly see what slips. Add a new project — see how it affects every other timeline. Lose an engineer — see which deadlines survive and which don't.

This is why we built simulations that run in less than 30 seconds. Not because simulation is a cool technology, but because you need to know the price of your decision in order to make the right one.

An educated decision isn't one made by smart people. It's one made by people who can see the consequences. If you're not being told what your choices cost, you're not planning — you're guessing.
