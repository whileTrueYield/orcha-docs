# Your Spreadsheet Knows More Than Your Chart

If you have one client, you can look at a chart. You can eyeball the trend, spot the inflection, make a judgment call. That's fine.

When you have ten thousand clients, charts are useless. You can't look at ten thousand charts. You need math.

## The Premise: All Data Comes From a Function

Here's the trick: if you assume your data was generated by some underlying function — and it almost always was — you can *find* that function. Or at least a good approximation of it.

That's what regression does. You fit a curve to your data. Linear, polynomial, exponential — whatever shape matches the behavior. And now you have something you can compute with, not just stare at.

## R² Tells You If It's Worth Looking At

Not every dataset fits a clean curve. Some data is genuinely noisy, some has regime changes, some is just random. You need a way to separate the signal from the garbage.

R² (the coefficient of determination) tells you how much of the variance in your data is explained by your regression. An R² of 0.90–0.95 means your fit captures the story well. Below 0.7? The fit is weak — maybe the data doesn't follow a simple trend, or maybe something structural changed midway.

Here's the power move: *make R² a column in your spreadsheet.* Now you can filter. Sort by R² descending, and the clients whose behavior is most predictable — and therefore most actionable — float to the top.

## First Derivative: Direction

Once you have a fitted function, take its derivative. The first derivative tells you the slope — is this metric growing or shrinking, and how fast?

That alone is more than most dashboards give you. A chart shows you a line going up. The derivative tells you it's growing at 3.2% per week. One is a picture. The other is a number you can sort, filter, alert on, and compare across ten thousand clients.

## Second Derivative: Acceleration

The second derivative tells you whether the growth is accelerating or decelerating. This is where it gets interesting.

A client whose usage is growing *and accelerating* might be going viral — or about to hit a scaling wall. A client whose usage is growing but *decelerating* is plateauing — maybe they've hit their ceiling, maybe engagement is dropping.

The chart looks the same in both cases — "line goes up." The second derivative tells you they're completely different situations.

## The Storage Prediction Trick

Here's a concrete example we've used: predicting when a client will run out of storage.

Take their storage usage over time. Fit a regression. If the R² is good (the behavior is predictable), find the function's intercept with their max storage limit — or with 80% of their max, if you want to warn early.

That intercept is a date. A specific date. "Based on your current usage pattern, we predict you'll hit your storage limit on October 14th."

This feels like magic. But it's just a regression, a fit quality check, and an intercept. The math is straightforward. And the R² tells you how confident to be — if the fit is strong and the pattern holds, that date is likely quite accurate.

## Automate Insight, Not Dashboards

The instinct is always to build better dashboards. More charts, more filters, more drill-downs. But dashboards are for humans who have time to look.

When you need insight across thousands of entities, the answer isn't better visualization — it's computation. Regression gives you the trend. R² gives you confidence. The first derivative gives you direction. The second gives you acceleration. Put them all in columns and suddenly your spreadsheet knows more than any chart ever could.
